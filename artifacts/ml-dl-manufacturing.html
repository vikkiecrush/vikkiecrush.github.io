ARTIFACT 03 Group Project Comparative Analysis Strategic Recommendation

Machine Learning & Deep Learning in Manufacturing

A group case-study comparing Siemens' Predictive Maintenance (traditional ML) with BMW's Automated Visual Inspection (Deep Learning), and recommending a phased AI-adoption strategy for manufacturers — delivered as an interactive HTML slide deck deployed to GitHub Pages.

My role: Comparativist + Strategist (Slides 8–11) · Group size: 4 members · Course: AIML 500

Introduction

This artifact is a collaborative case study produced for AIML 500 that examines how two of the most established AI paradigms — classical Machine Learning and modern Deep Learning — are being applied to solve real, high-stakes operational problems in the manufacturing sector. Rather than treating the two approaches as competitors, our group set out to show where each one belongs, why the choice matters, and how a real-world manufacturer should sequence its AI investments to earn maximum business value with minimum risk.

Description

The final deliverable is a 13-slide interactive HTML presentation, hosted on GitHub Pages, that walks a stakeholder audience through:

  • The industry landscape (data types, operational challenges, AI transformation)
  • A traditional ML case study — Siemens Predictive Maintenance (sensor-driven Random Forest / XGBoost models)
  • A Deep Learning case study — BMW Automated Visual Inspection (CNN-based image classification)
  • A head-to-head comparative analysis and critical trade-off assessment
  • A phased strategic recommendation (Crawl → Walk → Run → Fly)
  • An ethics and Responsible-AI reflection specific to manufacturing

My personal contribution to the deck was the Comparativist + Strategist role (Slides 8–11): synthesizing my teammates' individual case studies into an integrated comparative framework, arguing the strategic sequencing recommendation, and framing the ethical implications.

Objective

To produce a rigorous, decision-oriented comparative analysis of Machine Learning and Deep Learning in manufacturing — grounded in real corporate examples and peer-reviewed literature — that would give a non-technical executive audience a defensible framework for choosing between the two approaches and a clear starting point for their own AI journey. Success meant leaving the audience with a recommendation, not just a description.

Process

Our group followed a structured five-phase collaboration process:

  1. 1
    Role assignment & scoping — the four members divided ownership: Industry Analyst, ML Specialist, DL Specialist, and Comparativist + Strategist (my role). Each owner picked a real company as their case study anchor.
  2. 2
    Independent research — each member sourced peer-reviewed literature (Google Scholar, Consensus, Semantic Scholar), triangulated against official corporate sources, and drafted their assigned slide content.
  3. 3
    Synthesis (my portion) — I integrated the ML and DL case studies into a comparative framework across eight dimensions (business problem, data type, algorithm, feature engineering, interpretability, computational cost, ideal use case, ethical exposure), and drafted the phased adoption recommendation.
  4. 4
    Deliverable production — the group converted the written content into an interactive HTML slide deck (tabs, flip-cards, clickable roadmap, comparison-table toggle) rather than a conventional PowerPoint, to demonstrate technical fluency and improve stakeholder engagement.
  5. 5
    Deployment & review — the deck was published to GitHub Pages for team-wide access, peer-reviewed for accuracy against the source literature, and prepared for live presentation with per-slide speaker notes.

Value Proposition

“The most valuable AI advice isn't ‘use the newest model.’ It's ‘use the right model for your data, your team, and your risk profile.’ This project translates that principle into a phased, defensible adoption strategy — giving decision-makers a way to invest in AI without betting the factory on it.”

Unique Value

Most academic AI comparisons stop at “ML is interpretable, DL is accurate.” Our deck goes further — it prescribes a sequence. It argues that a manufacturer without mature data infrastructure should not begin with Deep Learning no matter how impressive the accuracy, because the operational cost, talent scarcity, and regulatory complexity will outrun the business benefit. Predictive maintenance with classical ML is the correct beachhead, and Deep Learning is the earned expansion.

The comparative-analysis and strategic-recommendation sections I authored show end-to-end business reasoning: taking two technical case studies and turning them into an executive-ready decision framework. That translation skill — from technical detail to strategic action — is exactly what distinguishes a data scientist from a Data Scientist who can lead.

Relevance

For recruiters and hiring managers, this artifact demonstrates three capabilities that scale beyond any single project: (1) the ability to synthesize independent research from multiple teammates into a single coherent argument; (2) the discipline to translate technical trade-offs into strategic recommendations executives can actually act on; and (3) the awareness to embed ethics and Responsible-AI considerations into the core recommendation rather than treating them as an afterthought. These are the exact skills organizations need as they mature from AI experimentation to enterprise-scale AI programs.
